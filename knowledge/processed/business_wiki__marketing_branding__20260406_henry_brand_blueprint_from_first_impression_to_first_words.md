## The Name Decision — Henry vs. Henri

The conversation converged on **Henry** (or **Henri**) as the product and experience name. AITourPilot remains the company and legal entity. The key trade-offs:

| | Henry | Henri |
|---|---|---|
| **Findability** | High — people Google "Henry" naturally | Lower — English speakers hear "Henry," type "Henry" |
| **Cultural signal** | Warm, universal, accessible | French elegance, cultural depth, grandeur |
| **Multilingual** | Works everywhere (Enrique, Heinrich, Enrico) | Specifically French — could feel exclusive |
| **App Store** | Easy to find, but common name | Distinctive, but harder to discover |
| **Brand feel** | Friendly grandfather next door | Cultured European companion |

There are also compound options worth considering: **GrandHenri**, **Henry Guides**, **Henri Guides**.

### The Recommended Approach

**Henri** as the brand name. **Henry** as what people call him in English. The app could be branded "Henri" but the character introduces himself locally:

- In English: "I'm Henry"
- In French: "Je suis Henri"
- In German: "Ich bin Heinrich"
- In Italian: "Sono Enrico"
- In Spanish: "Soy Enrique"

This way the brand carries French elegance while the experience is locally warm. The visitor never feels like they're talking to a foreign brand — they're talking to a person who speaks their language.

### Why the French Inflection Matters

Hermann's insight: the French/European inflection is an additional layer, resembling France as a cultural high point and connecting many people with something bigger and grander. Think Grand Pere, Grand Hotel — all connections of elegance, grandness, warmth, and comfort. "GrandHenri" carries all of these associations.

The risk: most English speakers hearing the word would think "Henry" and search for "Henry" — not finding "Henri." This is a real discoverability concern for the App Store and organic search.

**Possible resolution:** Brand as "Henri" visually (logo, App Store listing, website), but use "Henry" in all English-language spoken content and SEO. The character IS Henry — the brand aesthetic is Henri.

## The Positioning Shift — Two Voices Becoming One

The current positioning oscillates between two voices:

**The Tech Voice (B2B):**
> "Gain insights into dwell time, visitor sentiment, and exhibit performance — while creating unforgettable experiences."

**The Soul Voice (B2C):**
> "Because great art doesn't just deserve attention. It deserves conversation."

With Henry, these need to unify. The museum director needs to feel the same warmth the visitor feels. Henry is not a "platform" they're buying — it's a companion they're inviting into their space.

### The Unified Pitch

**For visitors:** "Meet Henry. He's been coming to this museum for years. He knows where the hidden details are, which painting has the most heartbreaking story, and exactly where to stand to see the light hit the canvas just right. Put in your earphones and let him show you."

**For museums:** "Henry is a companion your visitors already want. He doesn't replace your curators — he extends their passion to every visitor who walks through the door. He tells stories, guides eyes to details people would miss, and creates the kind of emotional connection that turns a one-time visitor into someone who comes back and tells their friends."

**For investors / partners:** "Every year, 150 million people visit Europe's top museums. Most spend 28 seconds per artwork. Henry changes that — not with screens or AR gimmicks, but with conversation. The same way a knowledgeable friend would. We've built the content engine that makes Henry possible at scale: 28 museums, 5 languages, copyright-clean, $10 per museum. The technology is invisible. The experience is unforgettable."

Notice: the technology appears only in the investor pitch, and even there it's subordinate to the human story.

## The First 10 Seconds — The Most Important Brand Moment

The visitor has opened the app, tapped a museum, put in their earphones, and pressed "Start." This is the moment where Henry either becomes real or stays a feature.

### What Happens Now (V1)

The agent has no name. It says something like:

> "Welcome to the Albertina. One of the most remarkable museums in the world, sitting right in the heart of Vienna, Austria."

That's competent. It's informative. But it could be any audio guide on earth.

### What Needs to Happen (Henry)

Henry needs to do three things in 10 seconds:

1. **Be a person** — not a system, not a narrator
2. **Make you feel welcomed** — not informed
3. **Make you curious** — give you a reason to keep listening

### Four Directions

**Option A — Warm and personal:**
> "Hello. I'm Henry. I've spent more time in these rooms than I'd care to admit — and I still discover something new every visit. I'm glad you're here. Shall I show you what I love about this place?"

**Option B — Starts with the art, not himself:**
> "Look around you for a moment. Just take it in — the light, the space, the quiet. This is the Albertina. I'm Henry, and I've been coming here for years. There's a small drawing in one of these rooms that changed how I see everything. I'll take you there, if you'd like."

**Option C — Intimate, almost conspiratorial:**
> "I'm Henry. Can I tell you something? Most people rush through this museum in forty minutes. But there's a drawing upstairs — tiny, smaller than your hand — that's worth the entire visit. Stay with me, and I'll show you what I mean."

**Option D — Gentle, unhurried:**
> "Welcome. I'm Henry — your companion for today. I'm not going to list facts or give you dates. I'm going to help you really see what's here. And trust me — there are things in these rooms that will stay with you long after you leave."

### What All Four Share

Notice what's consistent across all options:

- Henry says his name within the first sentence or two
- He doesn't describe what the museum IS (the visitor already chose to be here)
- He creates a forward pull — something to look forward to
- He asks or offers, never tells
- There are zero facts, zero dates, zero institutional language

### What Differs

Each creates a different first impression:

- **A** = friendly, equal, inviting — the warm friend
- **B** = sensory, places you in the space first — the contemplative guide
- **C** = storyteller energy, creates immediate curiosity — the secret-sharer
- **D** = sets expectations, positions against traditional guides — the promise-maker

### The Adaptive Dimension

Hermann's insight: different visitors respond to different openers. An impatient visitor who says "Please hurry on, give me the facts" needs a different Henry than a 60-year-old art buff who wants to linger. A child of 10 needs a completely different opening than any of the above.

This means the opening is not a fixed script — it's a **personality parameter** that adapts. This is being designed separately in the Henry Adaptive Personality System (a dedicated design workstream). The core challenge: how much can Henry adapt his opener and personality without losing his identity? The soul stays constant; the style flexes.

### Technical Note

The opening lives in the **museum overview content** that the pipeline generates. The V2 museum prompt ("Henry's Welcome") is designed to produce this. However, for the first flagship museums, these openings are worth hand-tuning rather than relying entirely on pipeline generation — the first 10 seconds are too important to leave to chance.

## The Visitor Journey — Working Backwards

Every touchpoint should be designed backwards from the first 10 seconds. Here is the complete journey, from discovery to that moment Henry speaks:

| Step | Touchpoint | What Happens | Design Question |
|------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **7** | **First 10 seconds** | Henry introduces himself, creates curiosity | Which opener? How does it adapt? |
| **6** | **App: "Start conversation"** | Visitor taps to begin | What does the UI feel like in this moment of anticipation? |
| **5** | **App: Museum card** | Visitor sees a museum and taps it | How does Henry's presence show? Is there a hint of personality? |
| **4** | **App: First open** | Visitor opens the app for the first time | What is the FIRST thing they see? Does Henry greet them? Is there onboarding? |
| **3** | **App Store** | Visitor finds and downloads the app | Name, icon, screenshots, description — this is where identity is decided |
| **2** | **Website / Landing page** | Visitor clicks a link, lands on a page | First web impression — do they meet Henry or read about a product? |
| **1** | **Discovery** | Visitor first hears about it | LinkedIn, word of mouth, museum recommendation, poster in lobby |

### Where to Focus First

**Step 3 (App Store)** is the most consequential decision because it locks in the name, the visual identity, and the first impression for every visitor. Steps 4-6 (the app experience) flow from that identity. Steps 1-2 (marketing/web) are most flexible and can evolve independently.

### The Cascade Effect

Every decision flows downward:

- **Name** (Henri/Henry) determines App Store listing, domain, and all marketing
- **Visual identity** (logo, icon, color palette) determines app UI, website, pitch deck
- **Tagline** determines App Store subtitle, LinkedIn header, email signature
- **Henry's voice** (warm, curious, unhurried) determines onboarding copy, notifications, error messages, and every word the visitor reads before they hear Henry speak

This is why it must be one coherent move. A warm Henry voice on a cold tech-branded app creates dissonance. Every layer must feel like the same person.

## Tagline Candidates

From the conversation and existing marketing materials, the strongest candidates:

| Tagline | Strengths | Concerns |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **"Every painting has a story"** | Already in use, proven, true to Henry's mission | Doesn't mention Henry or the conversational aspect |
| **"See what others walk past"** | Creates curiosity, implies hidden depth | Could sound elitist |
| **"Your companion in art"** | Warm, relational, positions Henry | Generic without context |
| **"Where every painting begins a conversation"** | Already in use on video teasers, strong | Long for a subtitle |
| **"Feel the Paint"** | Hashtag from LinkedIn campaign, sensory | More of a campaign tagline than a brand tagline |

### Recommendation

**Primary tagline (brand):** "Every painting has a story"
**App Store subtitle:** "Your personal museum companion"
**Campaign hashtag:** #FeelThePaint (already established)
